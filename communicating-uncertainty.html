Communicating Uncertainty

Whether in business, government, or organizations, communicating uncertainty is important for making informed decisions and maintaining trust.

What is Uncertainty?

Life is uncertain. While uncertainty can be reduced by gathering more information or consulting experts, it can never be entirely eliminated. Instead of avoiding uncertainty, we must find ways to communicate and manage it effectively.

Why Does It Matter for Communication?

Acknowledging uncertainty is crucial for trust. We should never pretend that outcomes are certain, as all decisions carry some degree of uncertainty. Misrepresenting certainty can lead to a loss of credibility when things do not go as expected.

Key Communication Strategies:

1. Make Uncertainty Part of the Message Show Example

  • Uncertainty should be part of the primary message, not just a secondary caveat.
  • The message should include the range of possible outcomes and their likelihoods.
  • If using quantitative expressions, clarify whether they are based on data or expert judgment.

Example: "Based on current data, we estimate that between 30-50% of people could be affected, though this may change as new information emerges."

2. Explain How Uncertainty is Managed Show Example

  • Communicate why a chosen option is a good one given the current state of knowledge.

Example: "We have chosen this policy because 80% of experts agree it is the best option given what we know. If new data suggests otherwise, we will reassess."
